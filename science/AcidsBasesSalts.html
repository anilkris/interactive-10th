What are Acids and Bases?

Acids and bases are two fundamental classes of chemical compounds with opposite properties. We can identify them using substances called indicators, which change color in their presence. This section provides a basic overview and a simulator to see how indicators work.

Acids

  • Have a sour taste (e.g., lemon juice).
  • Turn blue litmus paper red.
  • React with metals to produce hydrogen gas.
  • In aqueous solution, they produce hydrogen ions ($H^+$).

Bases

  • Have a bitter taste and feel soapy.
  • Turn red litmus paper blue.
  • In aqueous solution, they produce hydroxide ions ($OH^-$).
  • Bases soluble in water are called alkalis.

Understanding the pH Scale

The pH scale measures how acidic or basic a substance is. The scale ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral. A pH less than 7 indicates acidity, while a pH greater than 7 indicates a base. This interactive chart shows the approximate pH of various common substances.

Chemistry in Everyday Life

The principles of acids and bases are not just for the lab—they are at work all around us and even inside our bodies! From the food we eat to the way our bodies function, chemistry plays a vital role. Click on the cards below to discover the science behind these everyday phenomena.

Stomach Acidity

Why do we get heartburn?

The Chemistry

Your stomach produces HCl (pH 1.5-3.5) to digest food. Excess acid causes pain. Antacids like Milk of Magnesia ($Mg(OH)_2$), a mild base, neutralize the excess acid.

Tooth Decay

How do cavities form?

The Chemistry

Bacteria in your mouth break down sugar, producing acid. When the pH drops below 5.5, it corrodes tooth enamel. Toothpaste is basic to help neutralize this acid.

Bee Stings

Why does a bee sting hurt?

The Chemistry

A bee sting injects methanoic acid, which causes pain and irritation. Applying a mild base like baking soda ($NaHCO_3$) on the area provides relief by neutralizing the acid.

Acid Rain

How does rain become acidic?

The Chemistry

Pollutants like sulfur dioxide ($SO_2$) and nitrogen oxides ($NO_x$) react with water in the atmosphere to form sulphuric and nitric acid. Rain with a pH below 5.6 is acid rain.

Soil Health

Why do farmers test soil pH?

The Chemistry

Plants require a specific pH range for healthy growth. If soil is too acidic, farmers treat it with bases like quick lime ($CaO$) or slaked lime ($Ca(OH)_2$) to neutralize it.

Baking a Cake

What makes a cake fluffy?

The Chemistry

Baking soda ($NaHCO_3$) is a base that reacts with acidic ingredients (like buttermilk) or decomposes with heat to produce $CO_2$ gas. These gas bubbles get trapped, making the cake rise.
